An Introduction to 3D Printing

Over the past decade, 3D printing has rapidly emerged from a niche prototyping method into one of the most influential technologies in modern innovation. Originally developed for industrial design and engineering, it has expanded into countless fields as printers have become more affordable, materials more diverse, and software more accessible. Today, 3D printing shapes advances in manufacturing, medicine, aerospace, education, art, and even consumer hobbies—enabling faster development, customized solutions, and entirely new possibilities that were unimaginable with traditional fabrication. As the technology continues to evolve, its impact across industries only grows, driving creativity and redefining how we design and build.


Types of 3D Printing Technologies

3D printing has become one of the most versatile manufacturing tools available today. While there are many printing technologies, two of the most widely used consumer methods — FDM (filament printing) and SLA (resin printing) — dominate hobbyist and professional spaces. Each uses different materials and offers unique advantages.


FDM Printing

Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) is the most common and beginner-friendly form of 3D printing. It works by heating and extruding plastic filament layer by layer to build an object.

Types of FDM Materials

  1. Polylactic Acid (PLA)
    • Easiest filament to print; low warping and excellent beginner-friendly performance.
    • Made from renewable resources (corn starch, sugarcane); biodegradable under industrial conditions.
    • Produces smooth, visually appealing prints ideal for prototypes, models, and decorative objects.
    • Lower heat resistance than other filaments; not ideal for high-temperature or high-stress applications.
    • Average price: ~$15-$22 per 1 kg spool.
  2. Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene (ABS)
    • Strong, impact-resistant plastic ideal for functional parts.
    • Higher heat resistance than PLA; good for automotive and mechanical applications.
    • Requires higher print temperatures and good ventilation (emits fumes).
    • More prone to warping; benefits from an enclosure.
    • Average price: ~$18-$25 per 1 kg spool.
  3. Polyethylene Terephthalate Glycol (PETG)
    • Tough and flexible with great layer adhesion.
    • More durable and heat-resistant than PLA, but easier to print than ABS.
    • Slightly glossy finish; good for outdoor or mechanical parts.
    • Good chemical resistance (oils, solvents, moisture).
    • Average price: ~$20-$28 per 1 kg spool.
  4. Thermoplastic Polyurethane (TPU)
    • Flexible, rubber-like filament ideal for grips, phone cases, seals, and gaskets.
    • Resistant to wear, abrasion, and impact.
    • Requires slower print speeds due to elasticity.
    • Available in different hardness ratings (e.g., 95A, 85A).
    • Average price: ~$25-$40 per 1 kg spool.
  5. Nylon (Polyamide)
    • Very strong, durable, and abrasion-resistant; great for engineering and load-bearing parts.
    • Has some flexibility, preventing brittle failures.
    • Hygroscopic (absorbs moisture quickly); requires dry storage and sometimes drying before printing.
    • Performs well in gears, hinges, and mechanical assemblies.
    • Average price: ~$30–$55 per 1 kg spool.
  6. Polyvinyl Alcohol (PVA)
    • Water-soluble support material used with dual-extruder printers.
    • Ideal for printing complex overhangs, internal channels, and intricate geometries.
    • Prints best with PLA and low-temp materials.
    • Sensitive to moisture; requires airtight storage.
    • Average price: ~$30–$50 per 0.5–1 kg spool.

Resin Printing (SLA/MSLA)

SLA (Stereolithography) and MSLA (Masked Stereolithography) printers use UV light to cure liquid resin layer by layer. Instead of extruding plastic, they create extremely fine details with a vat of photopolymer resin.

Key Characteristics

  • Very high detail: Significantly smoother and sharper than filament prints.
  • Great for tiny, precise components
  • More cleanup required: Resin handling, washing, and curing add extra steps.
  • Brittle for some types: Though engineering resins are tougher, basic resin can break more easily than filament materials.
  • Average price: ~$25-$35 per kg

Applications of 3D Printing Today

Throughout the last decade, 3D printing has developed in sophistication and expanded into nearly every field of industry. 3D printing is now used across industries, from arts and hobbies to advanced engineering. Here are some of the most common and impactful applications:

Engineering

  • Rapid prototyping for product development and design validation.
  • Custom fixtures, jigs, and functional machine components.
  • Production of lightweight, complex geometries not feasible with traditional manufacturing.

Medicine

  • Patient-specific anatomical models for surgical planning.
  • Custom prosthetics, orthotics, and dental devices.
  • Biocompatible implants and experimental bioprinting research.

Education

  • Hands-on learning in STEM programs through design and prototyping.
  • Classroom models for science, history, and engineering concepts.
  • Encourages creativity and problem-solving through project-based learning.

Construction

  • Large-scale 3D printing of walls, housing components, and entire structures.
  • Reduced labor costs and faster build times through automated printing.
  • Ability to create complex architectural forms with less material waste.
